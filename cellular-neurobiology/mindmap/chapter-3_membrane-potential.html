Conductors

  • intracellular and extracellular electrolyte solution in cell
    • electric charge is carried by movement of ions in the solution inside and surrounding the cell
  • when electric potential difference (voltage) applied to the solution
    • cations(+) migrate toward cathode (-)
    • anions (-) migrate toward anode (+)
  • resistance of intracellular and extracellular electrolyte solution
    • depends on the geometry of the conducting volume
      • current flow can be constrained by the small volume of ICF and presence of organelles
    • also depends on the concentration of ions

Membrane conductance

  • total conductance of a membrane is the sum of the conductance of all ion channels that are open at the time of the measurement
  • underlying assumption: the interior volume of the cell and extracellular solution surrounding the cell is isopotential (same electric potential) 
    - gm = specific conductance 
	- conductance per unit area of membrane 
	- typical value: $g_{m}= 1 pS·\mu m^{-2}$ / $r_{m}= 1T\ohm·cm^2$
Ion channels are primary route of current flow across the membrane
  • specific conductance of membrane without protein is 1000 times lower than that of a typical cell membrane

Membrane Capacitance

  • within a limited voltage range the lipid bilayer behaves similarly to an ideal capacitor
  • capacitance is directly proportional to surface area
  • capacitance also depends on the permittivity and thickness of lipid bilayer
    - εr = dielectric constant
    $$
    F \propto \frac{z{1}·z{2}}{\epsilon_{r}a^2}
Water defect lower energy barrier for ion movement
  • lipid bilayer is a reliable barrier to ion permeation
  • lipid bilayer creates a large energy barrier for ion movement
    • relative permittivity of water is 80; relative permittivity of hydrocarbon core is 2.1
    • ions partition into the aqueous phase in very strong preference to the lipid phase making it very unlikely that ions will diffuse directly across the membrane
  • Water molecules can intersperse in to the structure → create water defect across the membrane
    • lower the energy barrier for ion movement across the membrane

Charge (q)

  • the fundamental unit of electrical function
  • Unit: Coulomb
  • charge on a single electron = elementary charge (e) = -1.602 E -19 C
  • z : the valence of the ion
  • Faraday constant → conversion factor (C->moles of electrons)

Electric potential()

  • the electric potential energy (U) per unit charge
  • The electric potential difference () = the difference in electric potential at two points 
    - Can be measure experimentally in voltage difference with a voltmeter 
	- one point is defined as ground (zero volts)
	- the electric potential difference is recorded relative to that point

Current (I)

  • the amount of charge flowing per unit time
  • Unit : Amperes (A)
    - 1 A = 1 Coulomb per second

Multi column

  • Resistance (R)
    - Unit: ohms ()

  • Good conductor has low but not-zero resistance

Ohm's Law

  • describes the linear relationship between voltage and current for and ideal resistor
I-V curve
  • a grave of the dependence of current flow on the voltage
  • an ideal resistor has a linear I-V curve
    • slope = 1/R or G
  • non-ohmic conductors are said to rectify
    • the conductance changes as the voltage changes

Kirchhoff's law

  • states that the sum of the currents flowing into a node of a circuit must equal the sum of current out of the node
    $$
    \sum {k=1}^n I{K}=0

Kirchhoff's Potential Law

  • the sum of the potentials across all the element in a circuit must equal zero
    $$
    \sum^n{k=1}=V{K}=0

Capacitance (C)

  • the ability of a capacitor to store an imbalance of charge
  • unit : farads(F) = 1 coulombs (C) / 1 volt (V) 
    - the amount of charge stored depends on the capacitance of a capacitor and the magnitude of the applied voltage
Current flow into or out of a capacitor

  • C= Capacitance
  • = rate of change of the voltage across the capacitor
    • if dV/dt = 0 → applied voltage is constant → no capacitive current flow
Parallel plate capacitor
  • current flow into a capacitor is stored as electric potential energy that can be released at some later time
    - the potential energy that produced current flow through a resistor is dissipated as heat
  • a sandwich of two conductive metal plates separated by an insulating material (dielectric)
  • Capacitance (C) of parallel plate capacitor
    • = permittivity of a vacuum
    • = relative permittivity / dielectric constant of dielectric material
    • A = surface area of capacitor
    • d = separation distance between the conductive plate
      • reduced in distance → reduced in voltage → increase in capacitance
      • E = electric field : independent of the separation distance
      • d is limited by dielectric breakdown
        • small d can cause dielectric breakdown when high current pass through
  • common used dielectric materials are insulator
    • low conductance but have a high relative permittivity
    • easily polarized

Electrical Components and Circuit

RC Circuit

  • Rm: Pooled conductance of the cell membrane
  • Cm: capacitance of the entire membrane
  • When current is injected, it flows either through the resistor or into the capacitor
    • current cannot flow through capacitor
  • current flow into the capacitance is proportional to the rate of change of membrane voltage
    • Beginning (high rate): all current flows into the capacitor
    • voltage across the circuit increase in proportion to the amount of charge transferred to the capacitor
  • Increase in Vm → increase fraction of current flowing through the resistor
    • expected from Ohm's law
      • describes the linear relationship between voltage and current for and ideal resistor
  • Current flow through the resistor is directly parallel to the changes in voltage
    • if the current is sustained → rate of change of voltage falls to zero
      • no capacitive current only a resistive current
    • At the offset of current step, charge stored on the capacitor flows through the resistor to ground
      • voltage declines slowly until all the charge has left the capacitor

Equivalent Circuit

  • each type of channel can be viewed as a separate conductance system

Model Cell with a Potassium Ion Conductance

  • most channel that are open at rest are Potassium channel
  • When Im = 0 (zero current), Vm = Ek (Equilibrium potential for potassium ions)
    • no current flow carried by potassium ions because the potassium ions are at electrochemical equilibrium
  • when membrane potential is more positive than Ek
    • ions flow out → outward current (positive current)
  • when membrane potential is more negative than Ek
    • ions flow in → inward current (negative current)

Cell with Potassium and Sodium Ion Conductance

  • typical membrane potential is more positive than potassium equilibrium potential
    - other ions in addition to potassium ions contribute to setting the membrane potential
    - sodium and chloride
  • Resting membrane potential
    - no net current flow across the cell membrane
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    I{k} +I{Na} &=0 \
    I{k} &= g{K}(V{m}-E{K}) \
    I{Na} &= g{Na}(V{m}-E{Na})
    \end{aligned}
^resting - driving force is considerably larger for sodium ions than for potassium ions at resting potential - Ik and INa are equal - because the *sodium conductance is much smaller than the potassium conductance* - shown by the shallower slope of the I-V curve for sodium ions ![[Pasted image 20250909235522.png]] ### Leak Conductance - Membrane conductance of a cell at rest is small - save energy by minimizing ion fluxes leaking into and out of the cell - Leak conductance → the sum of conductance of ion channels open at rest - the reversal potential of leak conductance is the set to the membrane potential ![[Pasted image 20250909235548.png]]^leakconductance
Membrane potential

The Goldman Equation

  • calculate the membrane potential of a cell when some, or all, of the ions are not in equilibrium
  • describe a relationship between the membrane potential (Vm), ion permeabilities, and ion concentration of the membrane permeant ions
  • = resting membrane potential
  • = permeability to the i-th cation; = permeability to the j-th anion
Ion permeability vs Ion conductance
  • related but not identical
  • both are determined by the number and types of ion channel found in the membrane
  • Permeability
    • only dependent on the properties of the membrane
    • not dependent on the number of ions in solution
    • it is a measure of the ease with which ions flow across a membrane
  • Conductance
    • dependent on the ion concentration
    • proportional to the rate of flow of ions through a membrane
    • determined from Ohm's Law

Resting cell membrane

  • the relative permeabilities depends on leak channels
    - predominantly K+ ion selective, non-negligible Na+ permeability
  • , relative permeability of membrane to sodium and potassium ions
    - typically 0.03
