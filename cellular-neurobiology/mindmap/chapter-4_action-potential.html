Action potential

  • an electrical event that is triggered by a depolarizing current stimulus
  • Transient voltage change produced by activation of voltage-gated potassium and sodium channels
    Electrically Excitable Cells
    • cells that can mediate an action potential
      - primarily neurons and muscle cells

Passive and Active Electrical Response

  • Passive Electrical Response
    • When a small current injected into an excitable cell
    • cell membrane behaves like the parallel RC circuit
  • Active Electrical Response
    • Firing of action potential in response to large current

Action Potential

  • a rapid rising and falling of electric potential
    • from -76mV to +45mV, taking ~ 1ms
  • The membrane voltage is driven by the conductance of voltage-gated sodium and potassium channels

Action potential in Axon

Self-propagating wave of depolarization
  • Route of electrical information
    • presynaptic nerve terminal → dendrites/cell body → axon → nerve terminal
  • Action potential initiates in the initial segment
    • rich in Na+ channels
    • depolarizing neighboring axon membrane → self-propagating

Passive Cable Properties

  • current inject into the center of axon can:
    • flow along the interior of the axon
    • flow back to ground across the membrane
  • The ratio of current flow along the axon vs flow across the membrane
    • depends on the membrane resistance relative to axial path resistance
  • Injected current will have a steadily decreasing influence on the membrane potential with increasing distance from the site of injection
Length Constant
  • the distance over which the steady-state membrane potential drops to 37% of the original amplitude
  • rm = membrane resistance
  • ra = axial resistance
  • conduction properties are the best when ra is small and rm is large
    • decrease axial resistance and increase membrane resistance
  • axon is not a very good conductors
    • voltage will decrement by ~95% within 1-2mm of the site of injection

Local Circuit Currents

  • the artificial injection of a current at one point cause current flow for some distance from the point of injection
    • distance depend on length constant
  • local circuit current flow acts to depolarize the neighbor cell membrane
    - membrane potential of neighbor region is pushed above the threshold by local circuit flow → initiating an action potential
one-way flow of action potential
  • this is achieved by the refractory period properties of action potential
  • the membrane region behind the action potential is in refractory period
    • unable to support a new action potential
  • Orthodromic action potential
    • action potential travels from cell body down the axon towards the nerve terminal
  • Antidromic action potential
    • action potential propagating in the opposite direction from the nerve terminal to the cell body

Factor Affecting Speed of Action Potential Propagation

  1. Membrane Resistance
    - Myelination → increase membrane resistance → increase speed

    Myelin forming cells (glial cells)
    • form a sheath around axon → acting as an insulator
      • reduce capacitance → increase resistance
    • nodes of Ranvier
      • periodic breaks in the myelin sheath
    • sodium channels clustered at the nodes of Ranvier
      • absent in the internodal membrane
      • Saltatory conduction
        • propagation of action potential leaps from node to node
        • conduction is fast between nodes and slow down in the node

    Myelinated axons are metabolic efficient
    • Ion channels clustered at the nodes of Ranvier
      • less energy required by Na,K-ATPase to maintain ion gradient
    • If channels are spread through the membrane
      • a lot of energy will be required to maintain the gradient because of the large surface area to volume ration in axon

  2. Axial resistance

    • increase diameter → reduce axial resistance → increase speed
      • usually limited because of space

Voltage-gated Ion Channels

Gating

  • the transition between the non-conducting and conducting states
    Why is gating important for channels?
    - ions flow down their concentration gradients faster than the Na, K-ATPase pump could pump them in or out of the cell
    - most of the ion channels remain in a non-conducting state at rest
  • Different stimuli the stimulate gating
    • membrane voltage
    • changes in intracellular Ca2+ and H+ ion concentration
    • binding of G-protein to the channel
    • phosphorylation
  • In most excitable cell, voltage dependent gating is the most important gating
Three states of voltage-gated channels
  • open, closed and inactivated
  • closed and inactivated state are functionally similar, but physically different
Rate of transition between states
  • depends on membrane voltage
    • rate constant that describe the transition is not constant
    • voltage-dependent rate constant
      - varies among different channels
    • At -80 mV, rate constant strongly favor transition to close state
      • most channels are closed
    • At +10mV, channels activate → move from close to open
      • If voltage quickly back to rest,
        • channels deactivate → move from open to close
      • If voltage maintained,
        • channels inactivate→ leave open state, enter inactivated state
      • voltage back at rest,
        • channels recover form inactivation → move from inactivated to close

Voltage-gated K+ channels

  • Structure of minimal K+ channel
    • Tetramers
      - Each subunit has three prominent α helices
      - 2 of them are membrane spanning: inner and outer helix
      - a pore helix located near the extracellular surface of the channel
      - both terminals located on the cytoplasmic side

  • Voltage-gated K+ channels are more complex
    • 6 membrane spanning domains
      • S5 and S6 correspond to the minimal K+ channel structure
Voltage sensor
  • S1-S4 domain - voltage sensor domain
    • S4 - contains 4 positively charged lysin or arginine residues at every third position interspersed with the hydrophobic residues
      • sense the change in membrane potential
    • upon depolarization -> S4 helix twists and moves upward
      - S4 moves through a hydrophobic 'gasket' formed by S1-S3
      - pulling the S4-S5 linker → triggers the the gate to open

Inactivation

  • N-type and C-type inactivation
  • N-type involved the N terminal of amino acid
    • "an inactivation ball" formed by the amino terminus of protein bind to the inner surface of the pore → clogging the channel

    - Na+ channels has a faster kinetic compare to K+ channels

Termination of action potential

    1. Inactivation of sodium conductance
    2. Activation of delayed potassium conductance
Afterhyperpolarization

  • K+ channels have a much slower kinetic compare to Na+ channels

Refractory state

  • an excitable membrane is said to be refractory when it either cannot support an action potential or when it can only do so if given a larger stimulus
  • Absolute refractory periods
    • Na+ channels inactivation gate close, K+ channels activation gates fully open
      • Na+ channels are inactivated → not ready to re-open
  • Relative refractory periods
    • K+ channels are still open, Na+ channels are beginning to recover from inactivation
      • there are enough closed Na+ channels that are available to produce an action potential
        • but a larger injected current is required because there are still K+ channels open
