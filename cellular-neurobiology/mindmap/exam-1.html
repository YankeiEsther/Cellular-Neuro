Enthalpy

Definition
  • U = the sum of the system's internal energy
  • p = pressure
  • V = volume of the system

    • forming a bond is exothermic
  • = endothermic = system gaining heat from the surrounding
    • breaking a bond is endothermic
  • enthalpy of a products and reactants of a reaction equals to their total bond energy
    • Exothermic reaction () -> when breaking a reactant with weak bond and forming a product with strong bond
    • Endothermic reaction () → when breaking a reactant with strong bond and forming a product with weak bond

Negative Gibbs free energy is favorable

Multiplicity

Why is there an equal number of solute molecules on both sides of the membrane at equilibrium
  • Thermodynamic explanation at equilibrium is statistical
  • when there are a large number of solute molecules, it is vastly more likely that there will be a even distribution of solute molecules between to equal sized compartments
    • Explained by the binomial equation

  • N = number of solute molecules in a given compartment
  • M = the total number of solute molecules
  • If N = 2 and M = 4
    • W = 6, meaning that there will be 6 different ways to equally distribute the 4 solute
    • vs N=4 and M=4 → W = 1, there only one way to distribute all molecules in one compartment
  • Each arrangement are equally likely
    - meaning that it is 6 times more likely to equally distribute than clustered all in 1 compartment
  • As M increased (molecule increase)
    • it become very unlikely that all the solute will cluster in one compartment
    • it become more likely that the solute will equally distributed between two compartment

Voltage-gated Ion Channels

Gating

  • the transition between the non-conducting and conducting states
    Why is gating important for channels?
    - ions flow down their concentration gradients faster than the Na, K-ATPase pump could pump them in or out of the cell
    - most of the ion channels remain in a non-conducting state at rest
  • Different stimuli the stimulate gating
    • membrane voltage
    • changes in intracellular Ca2+ and H+ ion concentration
    • binding of G-protein to the channel
    • phosphorylation
  • In most excitable cell, voltage dependent gating is the most important gating
Three states of voltage-gated channels
  • open, closed and inactivated
  • closed and inactivated state are functionally similar, but physically different
Rate of transition between states
  • depends on membrane voltage
    • rate constant that describe the transition is not constant
    • voltage-dependent rate constant
      - varies among different channels
    • At -80 mV, rate constant strongly favor transition to close state
      • most channels are closed
    • At +10mV, channels activate → move from close to open
      • If voltage quickly back to rest,
        • channels deactivate → move from open to close
      • If voltage maintained,
        • channels inactivate→ leave open state, enter inactivated state
      • voltage back at rest,
        • channels recover form inactivation → move from inactivated to close

Voltage-gated K+ channels

  • Structure of minimal K+ channel
    • Tetramers
      - Each subunit has three prominent α helices
      - 2 of them are membrane spanning: inner and outer helix
      - a pore helix located near the extracellular surface of the channel
      - both terminals located on the cytoplasmic side

  • Voltage-gated K+ channels are more complex
    • 6 membrane spanning domains
      • S5 and S6 correspond to the minimal K+ channel structure
Voltage sensor
  • S1-S4 domain - voltage sensor domain
    • S4 - contains 4 positively charged lysin or arginine residues at every third position interspersed with the hydrophobic residues
      • sense the change in membrane potential
    • upon depolarization -> S4 helix twists and moves upward
      - S4 moves through a hydrophobic 'gasket' formed by S1-S3
      - pulling the S4-S5 linker → triggers the the gate to open

Inactivation

RC Circuit

  • Rm: Pooled conductance of the cell membrane
  • Cm: capacitance of the entire membrane
  • When current is injected, it flows either through the resistor or into the capacitor
    • current cannot flow through capacitor
  • current flow into the capacitance is proportional to the rate of change of membrane voltage
    • Beginning (high rate): all current flows into the capacitor
    • voltage across the circuit increase in proportion to the amount of charge transferred to the capacitor
  • Increase in Vm → increase fraction of current flowing through the resistor
    • expected from Ohm's law
      • describes the linear relationship between voltage and current for and ideal resistor
  • Current flow through the resistor is directly parallel to the changes in voltage
    • if the current is sustained → rate of change of voltage falls to zero
      • no capacitive current only a resistive current
    • At the offset of current step, charge stored on the capacitor flows through the resistor to ground
      • voltage declines slowly until all the charge has left the capacitor

Diffusion

Diffusion of Solute - 'last mile' problem
  • Movement of solute throughout the body is achieved by either bulk flow or diffusion
    • bulk flow can move solutes over large distances within the body
    • Diffusion can only move solutes over relatively short distances with any efficiency
  • Last mile problem
    • it is relatively inexpensive to build the infrastructure (vascular system) to bring the solute close to its final destination
      • but very expensive to deliver directly to every cell
    • the last mile in delivering solute to cell is largely achieved by diffusion
      • efficient over short distance

Diffusion and equilibrium

  • diffusion of a small fraction of solute molecules can be very fast
    • but it can be very slow to reach equilibrium
  • Example: Calcium signaling in presynaptic nerve terminal
    • During action potential
      • there is a brief increase in membrane permeability to calcium ions
        • allows a relatively small number of calcium enter the cell rapidly → trigger neurotransmitter release

Diffusion and entropy

  • diffusion is an increase in entropy
  • As the entropy of the system increases the ability to perform work decrease
  • Clausius - entropy is that part of the energy which cannot be converted into work
    • there is a constant flux of energy into biological system
      • creating gradients of ions and solutes across the cell membrane
        • locally decrease entropy in the cell

Structure of K+ channel

  • Tetramers
    - Each subunit has three prominent α helices
    - 2 of them are membrane spanning: inner and outer helix
    - a pore helix located near the extracellular surface of the channel
    - both terminals located on the cytoplasmic side

  • Minimize energy barrier for efficient ion movement

    • a wide water-filled pore connecting to the narrow selective filter
      • K+ ion travel 70% of the channels while retaining their hydration shell
    • pore helices are positioned to maximize the helix-dipole effect
      • mimicking hydration shell → reduce energy barrier
Selectivity of K+ channel
  • highly selective for K+ over Na+ by a ration of 10,000:1
  • conserved amino acid sequence of selective filter: TVGYG
    • AA arranged to form a straight line, side chain facing out and carbonyl oxygen atom facing pore
      • forming five oxygen rings → four K+ binding sites
    • oxygen atoms facing the K+ ion replace the oxygen atoms from water molecule of the inner hydration shell
    • Na+ ion has a smaller ionic radius → doesn't fit as well into the selective filter → higher energy barrier
Ion permeation
  • two K+ passing each time due to electrostatic repulsion
  • channel cycle between two semi-stable states

Mechanism of Na, K-ATPase

  • two main state, E1 and E2
  • E1 state
    • ion binding site have high affinity for sodium ions
    • outer gate close, inner gate open
    • autophosphorylation caused large structural rearrangement → E2
      • during transition, both gates are closed
  • E2 state
    • binding sites have reduced affinity for sodium
    • outer gate open, sodium flow out
    • ion binding sites now have high affinity for potassium
    • auto-dephosphorylation mediated by hydrolysis with water molecules
      • conformational change → inner gate open
    • inorganic phosphate(Pi) initially remains trapped within the ATP binding pocket
      • release during transition back to E1 state, followed by binding of new ATP molecule
  • large conformational change limit the transportation rate

Channels

Simplest membrane transport proteins
  • solute can move in or out of the cell through the lumen of the channels
  • Generally specific for their function
    • prevent loss of ion gradients and influx of calcium
  • Three main types
    1. Ion channels
    2. Water channels (aquaporins)
    3. Gap junction channels

Ion Channels

  • usually specific to a subset of ions
    • anions or cations, or specific to only one particular type of ion
    • Specificity depends on the binding sites of the channel
  • Ion channels is also known as gating
    • have the ability to open or close in response to a specific stimulus
    • Leak channels - the channels that are open and passing ions at resting state
      • only a small number, predominantly K+ selective
  • Key role of ion channels - Generating electrical excitation
Why do we need separate channels specific for potassium and sodium?
  • The main function of these ion channels is to mediate current flow across the membrane to produce electrical activity
  • Potassium and Sodium flow through channel in only one direction
    - down their concentration gradient
    -Potassium channel mediate the outward flux of potassium
    - generating an outward current
  • Sodium channels mediate the inward flux of sodium
    • generating an inward current
  • Having two types of ion selective channels allow the cell to manipulate the membrane potential for electrical signaling or other purpose

Water Channels (Aquaporins)

  • selectively pass water molecules across the membrane
    • flow of water into or out of the cell is the primary determinant of changes in cell volume
  • generally always open
  • Regulate the water permeability of the membrane by increasing or decreasing the number of aquaporins

Gap junction channels

  • large non-selective channels
    • freely pass ions and small metabolites between cells
  • connect the cytoplasm of one cell to another
    • no net loss of small metabolites or ions from the cells
    • not normally open to the extracellular solution
