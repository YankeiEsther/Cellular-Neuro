Membrane Transport

Three major classes of transport protein
  1. Channels
    • facilitate diffusion of ions and water molecules down their concentration gradient by creating selective pores across the cell membrane 1
  2. Transporter
    1. facilitate movement of solutes down their concentration gradient
    2. or move solutes against their concentration gradient by using energy generated by moving another ion down its concentration gradient
  3. Pumps
    • use metabolic energy in the form of ATP to move ions up their concentration gradient

Common Properties of Membrane Transporter Proteins

  1. Integral membrane proteins
    • with multiple membrane spanning α helical domains
  2. Effectively 'wrap' the solute in a protein sheath creating a pore across the lipid bilayer
  3. One or more specific binding site are located in the pore
    • facilitating selective binding and passage of solutes

Number of gates

  • Ion channel: can function with one gates
  • Transporter/pump: require two gates to function correctly
  • only one gate can be opened at a time to prevent flow back of solutes
Transportation rate

Membrane Transport

Channels

Simplest membrane transport proteins
  • solute can move in or out of the cell through the lumen of the channels
  • Generally specific for their function
    • prevent loss of ion gradients and influx of calcium
  • Three main types
    1. Ion channels
    2. Water channels (aquaporins)
    3. Gap junction channels

Ion Channels

  • usually specific to a subset of ions
    • anions or cations, or specific to only one particular type of ion
    • Specificity depends on the binding sites of the channel
  • Ion channels is also known as gating
    • have the ability to open or close in response to a specific stimulus
    • Leak channels - the channels that are open and passing ions at resting state
      • only a small number, predominantly K+ selective
  • Key role of ion channels - Generating electrical excitation
Why do we need separate channels specific for potassium and sodium?
  • The main function of these ion channels is to mediate current flow across the membrane to produce electrical activity
  • Potassium and Sodium flow through channel in only one direction
    - down their concentration gradient
    -Potassium channel mediate the outward flux of potassium
    - generating an outward current
  • Sodium channels mediate the inward flux of sodium
    • generating an inward current
  • Having two types of ion selective channels allow the cell to manipulate the membrane potential for electrical signaling or other purpose

Water Channels (Aquaporins)

  • selectively pass water molecules across the membrane
    • flow of water into or out of the cell is the primary determinant of changes in cell volume
  • generally always open
  • Regulate the water permeability of the membrane by increasing or decreasing the number of aquaporins

Gap junction channels

  • large non-selective channels
    • freely pass ions and small metabolites between cells
  • connect the cytoplasm of one cell to another
    • no net loss of small metabolites or ions from the cells
    • not normally open to the extracellular solution

Na, K-ATPase

  • the most important transport system in mammalian cells
  • creates most of the nonequilibrium ion distributions
    • produce electric potential energy
      • critical for electrically excitable cells
      • ion gradient that facilitate secondary active transporters
  • single biggest user of ATP
    - especially in neurons because of the constant electrical activity and associated ion fluxes
  • Electrogenic pump
    • net movement of one positive ion out of the cell per cycle → electrical current
Mechanism of Na, K-ATPase

  • two main state, E1 and E2
  • E1 state
    • ion binding site have high affinity for sodium ions
    • outer gate close, inner gate open
    • autophosphorylation caused large structural rearrangement → E2
      • during transition, both gates are closed
  • E2 state
    • binding sites have reduced affinity for sodium
    • outer gate open, sodium flow out
    • ion binding sites now have high affinity for potassium
    • auto-dephosphorylation mediated by hydrolysis with water molecules
      • conformational change → inner gate open
    • inorganic phosphate(Pi) initially remains trapped within the ATP binding pocket
      • release during transition back to E1 state, followed by binding of new ATP molecule
  • large conformational change limit the transportation rate
Ouabain and Palytoxin
  • poisoned or inhibit pump → cell death
  • palytoxin bind to pump and caused open of both gates simultaneously
    • turning it into cation channel
    • dissipate a cell's ionic gradient

Ca-ATPase

  • Plasma membrane Ca2+ ATPase (PMCA)
  • Sarco/Endoplasmic Reticulum Ca2+ ATPase (SERCA)
  • actively pumping Ca2+ out of the cell
    • creating an approximately 1:10,000 gradient inCa2+ ion concentration gradient
  • In cardiac myocytes (cardiac muscle cells)
    • during cardiac contraction, Ca2+ channel remains open
      • Ca2+ ions enter cardiac myocytes
    • Ca2+ pumps return Ca2+ ions back to the lumen of the sarcoplasmic reticulum
    • high concentration of Ca-ATPase pump are required in the SR membrane to keep up with the Ca2+ release
    • Reduced of blood flow for a short period of time during heart attach can caused significant damage to cardiac muscle
      • fails to meet the energy demands of the Ca-ATPase pump

H,K-ATPase

  • closest related to Na, K-ATPase and function similarly
  • exchange two protons for two K+ ions
  • more limited distribution in the body
    • primarily involved in acid secretion by specialized cells in the stomach and kidneys

Osmolarity

  • a measure of the concentration of osmotically active particles in a solution
  • expressed as osmoles of solute per liter of solution
Osmolarity
  • For molecules that do not dissociate, e.g. glucose, urea, sucrose
    • 1 L of water containing 1 mol of molecules is 1 osmole/L
  • For solts or acids dissolved in solution
    • osmolarity depends on the number of ions the molecule will dissociated into
    • e.g. 1M NaCl solution is a 2 osmole/L solution
    • e.g. 2M CaCl2 solution is a 6 osmole/L solution

Regulation of Cell Volume

  • maintaining a balanced osmolarity between inside and outside of the cell is important
    • for maintaining integrity
    • and limit potential damage to the cell membrane
  • Osmolarity of the extracellular solution is kept within relatively tight limit
    • 275 - 295 mosmole/L in mammals
  • Changing of concentration of water outside of the cell will cause cells to shrink or burst
    • water flow down its concentration
    • if osmolarity inside the cell is much higher than outside
      • i.e. water concentration is higher outside than inside
      • water flow into the cell and causing rapid expands and dies

Water Chemistry

  • four pairs of valence electrons in water molecule
    • two involved in creating bonds between oxygen and hydrogen atoms
    • two lone electron pairs repel the two hydrogen atoms
      • creating a bond angle of ~105 degree
  • Difference in electronegativity
    • create a partial negative charge on oxygen atom and partial positive charge on hydrogen atoms
    • Polar molecule - polar covalent bonds
      • because of uneven distribution of charges

Intermolecular interaction

  • dipole-dipole interaction
    • the partial positive charges on H atoms form hydrogen bonds with partial negative charge on oxygen atoms of adjacent molecules
    • ~ 1/20 the strength of intramolecular covalent bonds
    • hydrogen bonds link water molecules together
      • form tetrahedral arrangement
    • water molecules' strong preference for interacting wiht each other
      • results in
        1. exclusion of hydrophobic molecules in aqueous environment
        2. hydrophobic molecules cluster together to minimize disruption of hydrogen bonding between water
          • self-assembly of lipid bilayer in aqueous environment

Deprotonation of Water

  • proton are unstable in aqueous solution and almost instantly react with surrounding water molecule
Electrical conductivity of water
  • Hydronium ions
    • contribute to the low electrical conductivity of pure water
  • proton hopping
    • protons jumping from one water molecules to another
      • creating current flow and movement of protons through pure water
    • Aquaporins, water channels, minimize proton hopping
      • preventing unregulated pathway for movement of proton cross the membrane

Concentration of water

    • concentration of H2O molecules in pure water is approximately 55.5M
    • if sugar molecules are added to pure water

Chemiosmosis

  • movement of ions down a chemical gradient to generate ATP
  • 10 H+ pumped across membrane for each pair of electrons passing through the chain
  • ATP synthase utilize the chemical potential energy to generate ATP
    • the structure of ATP synthase is conserved across all domains of life and apears to have been present in LUCA
  • Anaerobic respiration utilized similar mechanism
    • electron acceptor is not oxygen
    • less energy is generated by each electron transfer

Electron Transport Chain

  • generate gradient in hydrogen ions in all eukaryotes and most prokaryotes
  • catalyzes a redox reaction with an electron donor and an electron sink
    • the most common electron donor in eukaryotes is NADH
      • derived from digested food
    • electron transferred down the 3 protein complex via a series of redox reaction
  • Respiration is consider as more complicated in prokaryotes
    • prokaryotes perform both aerobic and anaerobic respiration
    • multiple electron transport chain may be used
    • proton gradient is generated across the plasma membrane
      • where eukaryotes generate the gradient cross the inner mitochondrial membrane

Lipid Bilayer Structure

Phospholipids

  • A hydrophilic head and a hydrophobic tail - aka amphipathic molecules
  • Lowest energy arrangement
    • hydrophobic tails point in towards the center of the bilayer
      • minimizing the interaction with water molecules
  • Barrier to the diffusion of many biological important molecules
    • interior lipid bilayer is almost impermeable to the diffusion of polar or charged molecules
    • hydrophobic molecules can pass easily
  • The hydrophobic core as a low relative permittivity (dielectric constant)
    • poor ability to store electrical energy when subjected to an electric field
